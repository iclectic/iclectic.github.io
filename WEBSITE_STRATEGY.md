## 1. Website Strategy

### Strategic Positioning
Ibim Braide is not simply a developer with a portfolio. He is a **software engineer, community organiser, open source contributor, and technical leader** based in the United Kingdom. The website must reflect all four pillars equally and position him as someone who:

- Builds production software across the full stack (frontend, backend, mobile, data)
- Leads and grows developer communities (Flutter Birmingham, Golang Birmingham)
- Contributes meaningfully to open source
- Thinks and writes publicly about AI ethics, engineering culture, and developer education

### Core Brand Principles
1. **Technically credible**: The site itself must demonstrate engineering quality. TypeScript, clean architecture, fast performance, strong accessibility, excellent SEO.
2. **Community-centred**: Community leadership is not a side note. It is a headline credential.
3. **Globally minded, locally rooted**: UK-based, internationally experienced (Nigeria, diplomatic upbringing, multiple countries). British English throughout.
4. **Thoughtful and human**: The tone is intelligent, warm, and considered. Not corporate. Not try-hard casual. Think: the person you would want to lead a working group or speak at your conference.
5. **Strategically structured for multiple audiences**: Every page must serve at least two of the five target visitor types.

### What the current site gets wrong
- **Positioning**: The homepage introduces Ibim as an "IT Support Specialist" who likes cooking and films. This undersells him dramatically. There is no mention of community organising, open source, or engineering leadership.
- **Narrative**: The About page is personal but unfocused. It reads like a casual bio, not a professional positioning statement.
- **Content depth**: Projects are listed as cards with descriptions, but there is no evidence of *thinking*: no case studies, no architectural decisions, no outcomes.
- **SEO**: Meta descriptions are placeholder text ("any description", "Generated by create next app"). No Open Graph tags. No sitemap. No robots.txt. No structured data.
- **Technical stack**: JavaScript (not TypeScript), no MDX, Three.js/R3F dependencies that are unused or decorative, large commented-out code blocks, a template-derived codebase ("developer-portfolio-starter-code" in package.json).
- **Architecture**: Flat page structure. No content system. Articles are hardcoded in a JS array. No dynamic routing for projects or articles.
- **Accessibility**: No skip-to-content link. No ARIA landmarks. No focus management on route changes.
- **Visual identity**: Gradient headings and indigo accents are pleasant but generic. The overall feel is "tutorial portfolio with good taste", not "senior engineer's personal site".

## 2. Information Architecture

### Principles
- **Progressive disclosure**: Lead with impact, reveal depth on demand.
- **Proof over claims**: Every section should contain or link to evidence.
- **Scannable**: Recruiters spend 30 seconds. The site must communicate value in that window.
- **Deep for the curious**: Conference organisers and Global Talent reviewers will read everything. Give them substance.

### Content Model

```
/                          Homepage (positioning + proof summary)
/about                     Full professional narrative
/work                      Selected projects with case studies
/work/[slug]               Individual project case study (MDX)
/writing                   Articles and essays index
/writing/[slug]            Individual article (MDX)
/community                 Community leadership and organising
/open-source               Open source contributions and philosophy
/speaking                  Talks, workshops, appearances (future-ready)
/contact                   Contact form + availability
/cv                        Downloadable CV + structured career timeline
```

## 3. Full Set of Pages

### 3.1 Homepage `/`
**Purpose**: First impression. Must communicate "who is Ibim Braide and why should I care" in under 10 seconds.

**Content**:
- Hero: Name, one-line positioning statement, subtle professional photo
- Three pillars strip: "Engineer. Community Organiser. Open Source Contributor."
- Selected work (3 cards, curated, not exhaustive)
- Latest writing (2 recent articles)
- Community highlight (Flutter Birmingham, Golang Birmingham)
- Trust signals bar (logos or text: BCU MSc, BCS, open source repos, community stats)
- Call to action: "Get in touch" or "Read my writing"

### 3.2 About `/about`
**Purpose**: The full professional and personal narrative. This is the page a Global Talent reviewer or conference organiser reads end to end.

**Content**:
- Professional positioning paragraph (who I am, what I do, what I care about)
- Career timeline (interactive, scroll-driven, more refined than current)
- Education (concise, not dominant)
- Values and approach to engineering
- Personal paragraph (the human side: cooking, reading, international upbringing). This is important but should come *after* professional credibility is established.
- Photo

### 3.3 Work `/work`
**Purpose**: Curated portfolio with filtering. Not a dump of everything. Only projects that demonstrate range, depth, and judgement.

**Content**:
- Category filters: Frontend, Backend, Mobile, Data, Machine Learning
- Each card: title, one-line summary, tech stack tags, thumbnail
- "Featured" badge for 3 to 4 key projects
- Links to case study pages

### 3.4 Project Case Study `/work/[slug]`
**Purpose**: Deep dive into a single project. This is what separates a senior portfolio from a junior one.

**Content (MDX)**:
- Overview (what it is, one paragraph)
- Context and problem
- My role
- Technical approach and decisions
- Architecture diagram or key code snippet (optional)
- Outcome and impact
- Tech stack used
- Links: live demo, repository, related writing

### 3.5 Writing `/writing`
**Purpose**: Establish thought leadership. Topics: AI ethics, software engineering, community building, developer education.

**Content**:
- Article index with tags/categories
- Featured articles pinned at top
- Each article rendered from MDX with proper typography, code highlighting, and reading time
- RSS feed

### 3.6 Individual Article `/writing/[slug]`
**Purpose**: Full article page. Must feel like a quality publication (think: a well-formatted blog post on a Vercel or Stripe engineering blog).

**Content (MDX)**:
- Title, date, reading time, tags
- Author byline with photo
- Full article body with MDX components (code blocks, callouts, images)
- "Related articles" at bottom
- Social sharing links

### 3.7 Community `/community`
**Purpose**: This is a unique differentiator. Most portfolios do not have a dedicated community page. It signals leadership, initiative, and ecosystem contribution.

**Content**:
- Headline: "Building developer communities in Birmingham and beyond"
- Flutter Birmingham: role, what you do, link, stats, photo if available
- Golang Birmingham: role, what you do, link
- Philosophy on community organising
- Testimonials or quotes from community members (if available)
- Call to action: "Want me to speak at your event?" or "Join our next meetup"

### 3.8 Open Source `/open-source`
**Purpose**: Demonstrate that you contribute to the ecosystem, not just consume from it.

**Content**:
- Philosophy statement on open source
- Featured contributions (repos, PRs, issues) with context
- Your own open source projects
- GitHub activity summary or link
- Call to action: "Interested in collaborating?"

### 3.9 Speaking `/speaking`
**Purpose**: Future-ready page for talks, workshops, and conference appearances. Even if empty now, having it signals intent and readiness.

**Content**:
- Headline: "I speak about software engineering, community building, and AI ethics"
- List of past talks (or "Coming soon" with topics you are available to speak on)
- Topics you cover
- How to invite you
- Photo for event organisers to use

### 3.10 Contact `/contact`
**Purpose**: Make it easy for anyone to reach you. Professional, not just a form.

**Content**:
- Short intro: "I am always open to interesting conversations..."
- Contact form (keep Formspree, it works)
- Direct email link
- Links to LinkedIn, GitHub, Twitter
- Current availability status: "Currently open to..." (remote roles, speaking, consulting, etc.)

### 3.11 CV `/cv`
**Purpose**: Structured, web-native career overview with downloadable PDF.

**Content**:
- Interactive timeline (experience + education combined chronologically)
- Downloadable PDF link
- Key skills summary
- Certifications

## 6. Visual Design Direction

### Inspiration
- **Stripe**: Clean typography, generous whitespace, subtle gradients, system-level polish
- **Vercel**: Dark/light mode done right, monospace accents, developer-native feel
- **Linear**: Restrained motion, precise spacing, one accent colour used with discipline
- **Lee Robinson's site** (leerob.io): Simple structure, MDX articles, developer credibility without visual noise

### Design Principles
1. **Typography-first**: Let the words do the work. Inter for body, a strong display font (Satoshi, Cabinet Grotesk, or keep Poppins but use it more sparingly) for headings.
2. **One accent colour**: Keep indigo (`#6366F1`) but use it surgically: links, tags, the occasional highlight. Not on every heading.
3. **Generous whitespace**: The current site uses `p-32` on Layout, which is good. Keep this discipline.
4. **Restrained motion**: Keep Framer Motion page transitions. Remove or simplify the Three.js blob. Use motion for entrance animations and hover states only.
5. **Dark mode**: Keep it, but ensure contrast ratios pass WCAG AA everywhere.
6. **No visual clutter**: Remove the floating "Hire Me" button. Remove the rainbow-cycling logo. Replace with a clean typographic logo or monogram.
7. **Card style**: Keep the current card approach for projects and articles but simplify the shadow/gradient stacking. One subtle border, one shadow level. No offset background divs.
8. **Photography**: Use your photo, but one clean portrait only. Not on every page.
9. **Grid system**: 12-column grid for layout. Max content width of 1200px. Comfortable reading width of 680px for articles.

### Colour Palette
```

Background dark:     #0A0A0A

```

## Appendix: Tone and Copy Guidelines

### Do
- Write in first person: "I build...", "I organise...", "I believe..."
- Use British English: organise, colour, analyse, programme (for TV), program (for code)
- Be specific: "I organise Flutter Birmingham, a monthly meetup for Flutter developers in the West Midlands" not "I run a meetup"
- Show, then tell: Lead with what you have done, then explain why it matters
- Be concise: Every sentence should earn its place

### Do Not
- Use em dashes anywhere
- Use buzzwords: "passionate", "rockstar", "ninja", "guru"
- Start sentences with "I am passionate about..." (overused)
- Use placeholder text in any meta tag or description
- Include personal hobbies before professional credentials
- Overexplain: Trust the reader to understand context

I organise Flutter Birmingham and Golang Birmingham. I write about AI ethics, engineering culture, and the tools we use to build."
